Not all profitable investments can be considered winners.

While evaluating one's investment performance, especially stocks, one should remember the golden rule that not all profitable investments can be considered as winners, because many a times a poor or an average investment can be overshadowed by sheer luck, making it look like a winner.

An example of this golden rule is Buffett's initial purchase of Berkshire Hathaway shares. This was highlighted by Buffett in his 2014 shareholder's letter.

1. In December of 1962, Buffett first invested in Berkshire via BPL (Buffett Partnership limited). At that time Berkshire was considered a pure value play as it was closing plants and using the savings to repurchase its stock. The stock was selling for $7.50, a wide discount from per-share working capital of $10.25, and book value of $20.20. "Buying the stock at that price was like picking up a discarded cigar butt that had one puff remaining in it. Though the stub might be ugly and soggy, the puff would be free. Once that momentary pleasure was enjoyed, however, no more could be expected."

2. Buffett kept buying the shares of Berkshire and by April of 1965, his partnership owned 392,633 shares, out of 1,017,547 then outstanding.

3.At the end of the 1964 fiscal year Berkshire's net worth had fallen to $22 million from $55 million from its 1955 merger. This was because of operating losses and share repurchases during the 9 year period. At this point the company had no excess cash and owed its bank $2.5 million.

4. Buffett was lucky.Berkshire immediately enjoyed two years of good operating conditions. Better yet, its earnings in those years were free of income tax because it possessed a large loss carry-forward that had arisen from the disastrous results in earlier years.

5. Then the honeymoon ended. During the 18 years following 1966, Berkshire struggled unremittingly with the textile business, all to no avail. In 1985 it finally closed its textile business.

BLUNDER#1 : Beyond Meat

I first came to know about Beyond Meat via my friend. Initially I thought it was just a hype. But when I first tasted its burger, I discovered its unique taste and soon discovered that it was capturing market share in a growing market of plant based meat.I waited for the stock to come down and made my first trade on May-1,2020 at $96.42 / share, in 19 days the stock reached my valuation and I sold it at $142.28 / share, pocketing 47.56% return in 19 days. Almost exactly after 1 year on 5/10/2021 , the stock was again down at my price level. I bought it at $106 / share and in 17 days the stock shot back to $138.28. I sold it, and made 30.45% return in 17 days.

THE GINNIE OF OVERCONFIDENCE OVERSHADOWED MY INTELLECT. I soon fell into the illusion that I had become the master of this new and upcoming industry. Even though the company was struggling to make any profit, I maintained my valuation of $140 / share assuming that eventually as the company captures market share, its margins would improve and will start generating free cash.

Fast forward 4 months, on Sep-16-2021 the stock was again down at $105.82 when I bought 10 shares. But this time, a different story started unveiling. By Nov-30-2021, the stock was down to $70. I was sitting on an un-realized loss of 35%. The market was seeing weakness in the economics of its business and was concerned that the company was not on track to grow its gross margin. I dismissed market's warnings and went on to double my position and bought 10 more shares at $70.68/share. Fast forward 5 months, I discovered I HAD MADE A BLUNDER. I was completely off my estimates on the company's gross margin.

On 5/11/2022 the company declared its Q1 results and I could witness my blunder. The company was struggling to grow its margin, thus was burning cash every quarter and its Balance sheet had just enough cash to run the operations for max 1 more year. What this meant was that the company was in the Penalty box. It either had to raise additional capital by selling more shares or raise more debt. Both options seemed tough because of the dwindling stock price of the company (had come down to $25 / share) and the rising interest rates. Thus I decided to unwind my position by selling the stock at $25.52/ share. Over all, in this 2 year journey of my wilderness, I had invested $1,765 in this stock and ended up loosing a net of $748.36.

BLUNDER#2 : Hertz Holdings

I bought Hertz an 4/27/2020 at $4.01 per share and sold it later that day at $4.30 per share, pocketing a return of 7.23% that day. In theory this looks like a great trade. But in reality I overlooked my most basic rule, that is a stock's underlying business should have AMPLE FACTOR OF SAFETY. I was wrong, the company shortly filed for bankruptcy on 5/22/2020. I could have easily had a permanent loss of capital.

I was just lucky to have escaped that and next time shouldnt expect this good luck. As they say in Hindi "Bakre ki maa kab tak khair manayegi". It means that good luck never lasts forever.

BLUNDER#3: Energy ETFs

This was the most rookie blunder one can ever do. In April-2020 as Pandemic had stalled everyone's life, Crude Oil prices touched zero and for some point even went negative. I thought of making a Trade that is tied to Crude Oil prices. Thus looked around and found the the oil ETF DBO. I didnt even bother reading the prospectus to figure out how they worked. I bought it on 4/15/2020 for $6.18 per share. On Apr-17 morning WTI dropped by over 8%, but DBO barely changed. I discovered that there are more nuances involved in calculation of DBO's share price. It was tied to the monthly crude oil futures. I committed the basic blunder of investing, which is to invest in something that one doesn't understand

MISTAKE#1 : Carnival Cruises

Bought the stock on 4/30/2020 at $16.03/share. Sold it on 7/30/2021 for $20.84 / share.

30% gain in 15 months. Seems good, but I am not satisfied, because I was way off in my initial estimates. Initially when I bought the stock, the stock was selling for deep discount because no cruise ships were sailing. My valuation was based on two core assumptions.

#1. The Company will be back to normal by July-2021.

#2. The Company will not need any additional Capital to survive.

By summer of 2021 I was wrong on both of these fronts. Thus wound up this investment.



MISTAKE#2 : Snowflake

This taught me a basic lesson that never be overconfident on something.

WHY I SOLD SNOWFLAKE PREMATURELY?

The basis of my valuation on Snowflake was my insight into the moat it was creating in the Datawarehouse world. It was purely based on my first hand use of the product and understanding the nuances of its architecture and how it had a great opportunity in replacing Oracle. But in few months I was able to experience as a user that its not all that rosy as promised. It was not saving the money to the extent it was promising. I realized that it will be be more than likely reflective in its results. But, the most important point that changed my mind was that the corporate world, until recently, was not keen on Oracle cloud. Now it has been open about it as it does save them the extra headache of re-writing their current applications. I can conclude that my confidence on Snowflake's moat has diminished drastically. It would be a success story, but not to the extent that I had thought and valued it at $150 billions. Overall I did make 20% on my investment. I would consider myself lucky because I had invested $2,792, almost 12% of my portfolio, and could have faced a major loss, I instead made a profit $565.80.

MY INITIAL VALUATION THESIS, Feb-2021 Snowflake might seem a super expensive stock. Because of my experience of working with the software, and seeing how it has been adopted by large companies, the stock seems undervalued. Its TAM (Total addressable market) is around $80 billions. Its current annual sales is around $500 million (Year 2020). Even if the Sales reach $10 billion, one can assume its value then to be around $150 billion. This multiple is similar to what other high end technology companies like Adobe are enjoying. Adobe's market cap (As of Jan-31-2021) is $220 billion with a share price of $458. Its 2020 annual sales was $13 billion. Snowflake's future multiple is somewhat conservative compared to that of Adobe.

MISTAKE#3 : Marathon Petroleum / Oxydental Petroleum

My analysis was pretty good on the above two companies. I also made the right move at buying them at a discount. My mistake here was that I chickened out and did not believe in my analysis and sold these stocks way before they reached my valuation price. This is one of the most common mistakes and is one that is to do with investment psychology. Well researched analysis is of zero value if a person starts second guessing it.

MISTAKE#4 : Las Vegas Sands (LVS)

Bought 10 shares at $49.80/share. Sold at $46.90 / share.Had a loss of 5.82%. This is the case of poor analysis. I valued the entire company with the same risk profile. I should have valued its USA operations and Macau operations separately. When I realized this, I sold it and took a loss.

WINNER : Extended Stay

Looking at all my realized trades, this is the closest that I can call as a winner. Other than the fact that I made a 66% return in 5 months and 18 days, my valuation was validated by Blackstone. They bought the company at the price I had valued. There is nothing more satisfying than the fact that your valuation is given a seal of approval by a leading Private Equity firm. But, I would admit that the timing did help me. My goal is not focused towards timing the market, but some stroke of luck is always refreshing.

OTHER INVESTMENTS My other investments like JP Morgan, Hartford Financial, Boeing proved profitable, but they all were helped extensively by wild forces of market. In one instance with IMAX, I had overlooked a major component on valuation. I later discovered and that my valuation model was inaccurate.

On 9/5/2021 I was reviewing IMAX's valuation, I noticed a fundamental mistake in my earlier valuation. I had missed considering financing receivables and the investments the company is making towards the equipments its leasing. These two factors change the valuation significantly. I decided to sell the stock as I could not depend on my earlier valuation.
